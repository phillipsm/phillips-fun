OpenBooth

In this case study we'll step through the process used to define the form and features of OpenBooth.

This documents efforts from 2017 to 2019 in Public Good Studio in Menlo Park, California.

Get a booth at OpenBooth.store.

Context

OpenBooth is a phone booth. A piece of occupiable furniture that creates an aural decoupling from surrounding chatter.

OpenBooth grew out of a project I started in 2016 at the Harvard Library Innovation Lab.

In 2018, I founded Public Good Studio to fabricate and sell OpenBooths. We currently manufacture OpenBooths in Menlo Park, California.

I acted as a Product Designer and an Industrial Designer on this effort.

OpenBooth is human-sized. The fully transparent front door offers privacy and security.

Problem

Open offices, libraries and similar working environments often lack single occupancy rooms that support phone calls.

In addition, the general construction costs associated with adding booths is prohibitive for many organizations.

We need a booth for phone calls that is fairly priced, and does not require a general contractor.

We no longer need phones in phonebooths, but we still need the privacy afforded by the booth.

Solution

The spaces for phone calls should be more like furniture than a permanent room - we want to avoid permitting, fire code concerns and construction expenses.

The result is a piece of occupiable furniture that can be shipped and relocated on site.

Step one

Generate a large pool of potential solutions. Go wild. Suspend disbelief.

Output will consist of 50 or so paper sketches and 3D models.

Draw many sketches - spend no more than 180 seconds per sketch. Think in wild ways. Let your imagination soar.

I made about 70 sketches and models that explored different fundamental starting points for OpenBooth.

Question the fundamentals. What is a phone booth?

3D modeling in low-fidelity quickly advances your thinking beyond a flat plane.

This model wanted a sliding, wrap-around door.

Step two

Gather similar ideas and approaches. Push for new possibilities.

Output will be a set of inspirational photos and mockups of rough shapes.

We want to take the most promising ideas and develop them a little. Let's follow our noses with designs that go beyond paper and pencil.

We're still adding and expanding in this step. Continue to suspend disbelief. Avoid focusing on details, things like construction methods and materials are beyond scope.

Getting the feel of working in a booth.

Should we provide a desk? A stool?

Step three

Sketch low-fidelity interfaces. Examine prior art.

Output will consist of sketched views and simple scale models.

We've reached the middle of the process and now we start to narrow our focus. Let's look at prior art and begin moving with intention.

Our minds should be focused on synthesizing and playing with some of the more interesting ideas that have emerged.

I used a laser cutter to make 1:12 size paper models of a few interesting elevations.

Sketching out exciting features leads to working knowledge of the potential object.

Step four

Refine and focus. Validate.

Output will consist of one or two medium-fidelity wireframes.

Once we have a feel for our object, we can begin the great translation. Time to dig into model work and form find. We want to think about features that shape the use of the booth.

It's a critical time to catch project-blocking bugs by using CAD modeling software - transcribed paper sketches into 3D CAD parts and grouped those parts into five subassemblies.

Elevations of potential booth shapes have been drawn on the wall with orange tape.

Another pass at 1:12 paper models. Thicker paper allowed me to test desks.

Human shapes give scale to these 7" models.

Schematic drawings of potential booth subassemblies.

Step five

Documentaiton, prototyping and handoff.

Output will consist of scale prototypes and model documentation.

As we wrap up the design process, we want to create artifacts that will standardize our booth builds. Construction documentation and notes on machining and materials are core.

We'll also start to share the visual assets that will be used in marketing and support materials.

Building a 1:2 model revealed that I had way too many unique pieces in the model.

I made three 1:2 models - each one was a little under four feet tall.

Finished

We've wrapped up the design process and have scale models that serve as reference as we begin the production builds.

Hardware is hard. We'll need to iterate many times to find efficiencies in our builds.

These demo booths ready to provide privacy and focus.

Tools

I used SolidWorks 2018 to generate CAD models, and Autodesk's HSMWorks to generate toolpaths in CAM. I fabricated booths with a 3 axis CNC router controlled by LinuxCNC.

Adobe Illustrator and Photoshop were used to generate documentation. GitHub and Jekyll power this portfolio site.
