Perma Case Study

In this case study we'll walk through the process used to define the interface that displays web archives at Perma.cc.

This feature was implemented in 2017 in the Harvard Library Innovation Lab in Cambridge, MA.

Create your own web archives at Perma.cc.

Context

Perma.cc is a web service that helps scholars, journals, courts, and others create permanent records of the web sources they cite.

It's used by more than half of the law schools in North America, has been used by a US President and the Supreme Court.

Perma's core service is simple - input a URL and receive an archive in return. Your archive is stored at an academic or public library and is available forever. No more 404s!

I played the roles of Interaction Designer, Full Stack Developer and Product Designer on the Perma.cc team from 2015 to 2017.

Enter any URL and Perma.cc will generate a web archive for you

Problem

Presenting an archive of a webpage is a fine balance - you want give the maximum view to the archive replay, but you also need to make it clear that the images and text being displayed are a snapshot from another website.

The user is seeking the archive replay and that's it - let's add only the most necassary labels and admin buttons.

The web page Perma.cc shows you is a recreation of the original. An archive.

Solution

The interface we arrived at uses a thin blue header with a couple of dropdown panels to house admin control and archive metadata.

We found that first-time users could quickly navigate and comprehend this pattern. And, it met our engineering requirements of being efficient across devices and displays.

The end result of this design process and the interface currently in production.

Process

This process is an iterative one. It guides us through a broad range of paths forward and helps avoid arriving at a local maximum.

The core thing we're doing here is turning up the variance. We want to produce wild interfaces and then cull the set.

This process can be widely applied to software and hardware enhancements. It's served me well for many years over many efforts.

Avoid local maximums through wildness

Step 1

Generate a large pool of potential solutions. Go wild. Suspend disbelief.

Output stacks of index cards.

Gather two to a few people around a table and quikcly pitch the problem to be solved. Coach the group a little by telling them that this step of the process should additive and encouraging - it's not the time to think critically.

Set a timer for five minutes and ask each person to rapidly generate solutions by writing text and drawing sketches on index cards.

After the five minute timer rings, ask each person to briefly describe the 3 to 8 cards they've generated. Each round of sketch/share should last no more than 15 minutes total.

Conduct three or so rounds. Keep going for an hour or two until the energy starts to curve down.

You'll end up with mostly bad ideas, with a few potential gems mixed in. You want to keep a light mood that's freewheelin and one that resembles an improv comedy performance.

Step 4

Refine and focus.

Refine and focus Canvas power users, coworkers, test partners and friends to play with sketches. This step not only refines and stress tests potential directions, it gets the team on the same page. It clarifies. It provides focus on the best path forward.

Output wireframes, user feedback, focused plan

Step 5

Prototype and build out

Prototype and test Prototype and revisit the users from the previous step Friendly users see an InVision style mockup. If that's touch, they see the feature branch that's been built out by the engineers. Take lessons learn. Iterate on any deal breakers and roll out the feature. Build support docs and help the communications team get the word out.
